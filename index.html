This is the story of how building one product changed the way I think about designing for people.

About me

My Journey

Before I ever wrote code, I spent more than 10 years as a Dental Assistant. Every day I worked with people who were nervous, uncomfortable, or unsure of what was coming next. I learned that listening, earning trust, and explaining things clearly could completely change someone's experience.

When I transitioned into software through Fair Chance Futures, I realized those same ideas applied to product design. As I built with React, FastAPI, Python, and AI, and contributed to CesiumJS through open source, I kept noticing that people do not just remember whether something worked. They remember how it felt to use.

That background shaped how I see design. My favorite part is turning feedback into experiences that make products easier and more enjoyable to use.

The question that guided us

How can AI help students learn without taking the learning away?

I started by thinking about what features students needed. Through testing ideas, gathering feedback, and iteration, I realized the challenge was not helping students get answers. It was helping them stay engaged long enough to discover the answers themselves.

Three moments that shaped the product

The decisions that changed how it felt to use.

Once I understood the problem differently, the design decisions changed too. These were the moments where my thinking moved from building features to building confidence.

Giving students control

Assumption: Students should move through guidance in a fixed order.

What changed: As I tested the experience, I realized students did not all need the same path. Some only wanted a small hint, while others wanted more guidance.

Result: Letting students choose made the experience feel more supportive and less restrictive.

Building trust

Assumption: More detail would automatically make the instructor view more useful.

What changed: I had to think about instructor insight alongside the student's need to ask questions safely.

Result: Showing trends without names helped instructors see where support was needed without making students feel watched.

First Impression

Assumption: The login screen was just a way to enter the product.

What changed: Designing the animated door reminded me that people begin forming opinions about a product before they ever click their first button.

Result: It made the first moment feel more intentional, like entering a space built for learning.

What I'll carry forward

Design is really about understanding people.

Before Our Door, I thought good products were mostly about building useful features. This project changed that. Clearer wording, reducing uncertainty, listening to feedback, and giving people more control can matter more than adding something new. It helped me understand why I enjoy product design: I like improving something until it feels easier, calmer, and more natural for the people using it. More than anything, Our Door showed me that thoughtful design does not just help people complete a task; it helps them feel confident enough to keep going.
